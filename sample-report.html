Executive summary

The product feels thoughtful; the first screen makes visitors work too hard to understand why it matters.

Northstar appears to have a distinct, premium product and a visually calm brand. The above-the-fold experience, however, leads with atmosphere before customer value. A new visitor has to infer the product type, the outcome, and the reason to act now.

The fastest opportunity is not a wholesale redesign. It is clarifying the message, reducing choice friction, and making the proof already available easier to notice.

Primary opportunityMake the first 10 seconds answer: what is this, who is it for, and why choose it today?

In this audit

  • The hero headline is evocative but does not name the product or the outcome.
  • The primary call to action competes with two equally weighted next steps.
  • Trust signals exist below the fold, after the visitor has already been asked to buy.

Prioritized opportunities

Fix the friction closest to the decision.

Prioritized by likely downside, not by cosmetic preference.

01critical

Hero message hides the product benefit

Impact
High: unclear relevance can cause immediate exits from cold traffic.
Effort
small

What we observed: The hero uses lifestyle language and a product image, but does not state that Northstar sells guided journals or what makes the system useful.

Recommended move: Lead with the customer outcome and product category in plain language, then keep the existing emotional tone in the supporting sentence.

Evidence
  • The first descriptive product phrase appears after the initial call to action.
  • The headline does not include a customer problem, outcome, or product category.
02high

Calls to action split the decision

Impact
Medium-high: multiple equal actions dilute the intended purchase path.
Effort
small

What we observed: Shop, explore prompts, and take the quiz are presented with similar visual weight in the first viewport.

Recommended move: Choose one primary CTA for purchase-intent visitors and demote exploration paths to a secondary text treatment.

Evidence
  • Three first-screen actions have button-like styling.
  • No copy explains which option is the best first step.
03high

Proof arrives after the ask

Impact
Medium: visitors have little reason to trust the promise before seeing price or purchase controls.
Effort
medium

What we observed: Customer testimonials and the product methodology are separated from the hero by several content blocks.

Recommended move: Add one concise proof point near the main CTA, with a link to deeper reviews or the methodology.

Evidence
  • The first social-proof element is not visible in a standard desktop first viewport.
  • The founder or methodology story is available but not summarized beside the offer.
04medium

Product selection begins without a guide

Impact
Medium: an unfamiliar catalog can create hesitation for first-time shoppers.
Effort
medium

What we observed: The collection presents several journal types without a short comparison or recommended starting point.

Recommended move: Name the best first product, add a one-line chooser, and make any quiz clearly optional.

Evidence
  • Journal names do not indicate the intended use case at a glance.
  • The catalog does not visibly identify a popular or starter option.

Experiment queue

Five hypotheses worth testing.

Each should be tested against a defined baseline before rollout.

01

Naming the product and outcome in the headline will improve product-page starts from new visitors.

Test: A/B test the rewritten headline against the current lifestyle-first headline.

Win signal: Increase in click-through rate on the primary CTA, segmented by first-time visitors.

02

A single dominant CTA will reduce decision friction.

Test: Keep ‘Find your first journal’ as the only filled hero button; render other paths as links.

Win signal: Higher primary-CTA share without a drop in downstream add-to-cart rate.

03

Visible proof beside the CTA will increase confident product exploration.

Test: Add a short verified-review excerpt or methodology statistic under the hero CTA.

Win signal: Lift in product detail views and add-to-cart rate from homepage sessions.

04

A named starter recommendation will help unfamiliar visitors choose.

Test: Feature one ‘Start here’ journal with its intended outcome in the collection entry point.

Win signal: Higher collection-to-product click-through and lower collection-page exits.

05

A lightweight shipping reassurance will lower last-mile uncertainty.

Test: Place a truthful delivery/returns note below the hero CTA.

Win signal: Improved add-to-cart rate; monitor support questions to confirm it addresses a real concern.

Deliverable: One-page baseline and event checklist.

Day 2

Message

Deliverable: Final copy block and claim-approval notes.

Day 3

First-screen build

Implement the hero variant, secondary links, and one proof element in a reversible test or staging environment.

Deliverable: QA-ready test variant.

Day 4

Selection help

Add a starter recommendation or concise use-case labels to the entry collection.

Deliverable: Annotated collection experience.

Day 5

Measurement

Verify analytics events, test device layouts, and define the minimum sample and decision rule before launch.

Deliverable: Experiment measurement plan.

Day 6

Launch

Release the first controlled variant and check for broken links, mobile regressions, and unexpected event changes.

Deliverable: Live test with launch checklist.

Day 7

Read signal

Review early directional data and qualitative session notes; retain, revise, or stop the variant based on the pre-set rule.

Deliverable: Short learning memo and next experiment decision.
